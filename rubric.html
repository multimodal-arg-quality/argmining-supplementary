Quality Scoring Rubric

Three dimensions assess how effectively a visualization’s design supports its argumentative function, grounded in the L1–L4 semantic content hierarchy. Each chart receives a score of 1–5 on every dimension.

L1–L4 Semantic Content Foundation

Following Lundgard & Satyanarayan (2021), we characterize each chart’s visual evidence at four semantic levels. Our three scoring dimensions then measure specific alignments within this hierarchy.

LevelContentExample
L1 Chart structure & encodings Grouped bar chart; x-axis = forcing agent, y-axis = W/m²
L2 Statistical & relational patterns CO&sub2; bar is tallest positive; aerosol bar is tallest negative
L3 Perceptual salience Color-coded bars make CO&sub2; dominance immediately visible
L4 High-level semantic meaning “CO&sub2; is the dominant anthropogenic forcing agent”

Composite Formula

Q = (Align + WStr + SRel) / 3

All three dimensions are equally weighted. The composite score Q ranges from 1 to 5.
Each dimension measures a specific alignment within the L1–L4 hierarchy, as shown in the rubric table below.

Dimension Rubric

Dimension L1–L4 Alignment Score 1 (Poor) Score 2 (Weak) Score 3 (Adequate) Score 4 (Strong) Score 5 (Excellent)

Dimension Details

Align (Claim–Evidence Alignment) — L2 ↔ L4

Assesses whether the statistical and relational patterns visible in the chart (L2) directly depict the high-level claim stated in the caption (L4). Perfect alignment means the chart is the canonical visual depiction of the stated claim—for instance, a claim about “diverging trends” paired with clearly diverging lines. Misalignment means the chart shows something tangential to or different from the claim.

WStr (Warrant Strength) — L1 → L2 → L3 → L4 chain

Evaluates the coherence and recoverability of the full cross-modal reasoning chain from chart structure (L1) through statistical patterns (L2) and perceptual salience (L3) to the semantic claim (L4). Strong warrants allow the reader to readily articulate why the evidence supports the claim—the inferential step from seen pattern to stated proposition becomes easy to reconstruct. Weak warrants leave the reasoning opaque or require speculative leaps.

SRel (Salience–Relevance Match) — L3 ↔ L4

Measures whether the most perceptually salient features of the chart (L3) are also the most argumentatively relevant to the claim (L4). A high score means the chart’s design naturally directs attention to the evidence supporting the claim (e.g., color-coding the key comparison). Low scores indicate visual distractors that compete with or suppress the relevant evidence.
