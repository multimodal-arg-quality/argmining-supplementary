Quality Scoring Rubric

Five dimensions assess how effectively a visualization's design supports its argumentative function. Each chart receives a score of 1–5 on every dimension.

Composite Formula

Q(G) = Compgate × [ 0.20 × Align + 0.30 × WStr + 0.30 × SRel + 0.20 × CBudget ] / 5

Compgate: If Completeness ≤ 2, multiply final score by 0.5 (penalty for incomplete Toulmin structure). Otherwise Compgate = Comp raw score.
WStr and SRel are weighted 0.30 each because they capture the uniquely multimodal dimensions of argument quality.
The denominator of 5 normalizes the result to a 0–5 scale.

Dimension Descriptions

Dimension Score 1 (Poor) Score 2 (Weak) Score 3 (Adequate) Score 4 (Strong) Score 5 (Excellent) Weight

Dimension Details

Comp (Completeness)

Measures how many of the six Toulmin components (Claim, Evidence, Warrant, Backing, Qualifier, Rebuttal) are identifiable in the visualization–caption pair. This acts as a gate: incomplete arguments receive a penalty multiplier. A chart that only encodes a claim and evidence but lacks any recoverable warrant scores low, regardless of how well-designed it is.

Align (Claim-Evidence Alignment)

Assesses the semantic match between the textual claim in the caption and the visual evidence in the chart. Perfect alignment means the chart is the canonical visual depiction of the stated claim—for instance, a claim about “diverging trends” paired with clearly diverging lines. Misalignment means the chart shows something tangential to or different from the claim.

WStr (Warrant Strength)

Evaluates the quality of the cross-modal reasoning link that connects visual evidence to the textual claim. This is the uniquely multimodal bridge: how does the reader infer the claim from what they see? Strong warrants involve explicit, scheme-appropriate reasoning (e.g., comparative warrant for threshold-crossing claims). Weak warrants are speculative or logically unsound.

SRel (Salience-Relevance Match)

Measures whether the most visually salient features in the chart are also the most argumentatively relevant. A high score means the chart’s design naturally directs attention to the evidence supporting the claim (e.g., color-coding the key comparison). Low scores indicate visual distractors that compete with or suppress the relevant evidence.

CBudget (Complexity Budget)

Assesses how well the chart manages visual complexity relative to the argument’s demands. Charts with high information density can score well if they provide scaffolding (annotations, labels, visual grouping) that guides the reader. Overwhelming charts with no guidance score poorly. Simple charts that are immediately graspable score highest.

Tier Boundaries

Top
Q(G) ≥ 3.5
Mid
2.5 ≤ Q(G) < 3.5
Bottom
Q(G) < 2.5
